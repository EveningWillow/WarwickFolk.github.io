A little introduction...

Who are we?

We are a collection of musicians, dancers, and generally curious individuals who appreciate the various folk traditions that exist in Britain and beyond.

All are welcome - whether you are a seasoned attendee of ceilidhs, an instrumentalist looking to branch out or just generally looking for something that is both new and traditional, Warwick Folk is here for you.

No experience needed (fun fact I had never played folk before coming to university and knew very little about British folk culture but the society provided a lovely relaxed insight into a really fun tradition).

What we do?

We play and dance to a large array of traditional folk music and perform live throughout the year. We also periodically host Ceilidhs for all to join in the fun.

What do we stand for? (i.e. society constitution)

Warwick Folk is an inclusive society that seeks to ensure the enjoyment of all its members. All skill levels are welcome and will be accommodated. We endeavour to provide the best conditions for sharing the folk traditions.

    Learning about Folk

    Types of tunes

    Generally, folk tunes are defined by the kinds of dances they are set to. Thus, all the tunes we play are also terms for kinds of traditional folk dance.

    • Reels: A term which refers to a tune in simple metre. These tunes are very diverse but are generally pretty understandable for a newcomer.
    • Polkas: Often have swing feel to them and are played fast.
    • Jigs: A lively and bouncy type of tune most often associated with Ireland often calls for an energetic dance.
    • Slip Jigs: Similar feel to a jig, but is played in 9/8, giving it an uneven quality.
    • Hornpipes: A type of tune/dance most often associated with sailors and is typically with an uneven feel.
    • Waltzes: Played in 3/4 (counted in threes). Generally played fairly slowly and can often be played quite expressively.
    • Mazurkas: Also played in 3/4. Often livelier than a waltz and with the second or third beats accented (as opposed to the usual accented first beat).
    • Slides: Played in 12/8. Slides have a similar feel to jigs, but with more flowing melodies.
    • Playfords: A collection of tunes and dances written by John Playford, who attempted to bring folk dancing to the higher classes in the 1650s.

    Contribute

    Musical contributions

    You can contribute to the Band Folder by going to the Band Folder repository. You will also find guidance and instructions as how to use abc (common folk music notation system) there.

    Any new tune suggestions and new sets can be submitted at weekly band practice or by contacting a member of the exec (via Facebook or email). The webmaster will add it to the page for distribution.
